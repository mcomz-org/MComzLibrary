# MComz Literature

*Twenty-seven classic works chosen so that someone reaching for a book in hard times is rewarded for the choice.*

## Keep reading.

In a crisis, people still need stories. They pass the hours that have to pass. They get you out of your own head. And they remind you — when you need reminding — that other people have been through worse and come back able to tell you about it. This collection is for those hours.

We couldn't include modern fiction (see below), and we couldn't include everyone's favourites. What we have done is assemble a shelf of durable, publicly distributable works across the moods a reader needs — morale, philosophy, play, wonder, adventure — prioritising books that have already survived the selection pressure of long reading.

## The books

Click any title to open the text. Every book is public domain. We've drawn from Standard Ebooks (professionally typeset, proofread) wherever possible, and Project Gutenberg elsewhere. All texts are full-text searchable.

## Where we stopped, and why

We stopped where five conditions converge:

1. **The reader comes first.** This collection is used in emergency contexts, often by people who are not yet habitual readers. Every book has to stand on its own — to reward someone who opens it cold, without already knowing the author or the reputation. Representation across authors and traditions matters, and is reflected in the priorities of the shortlist below. But we will not add a work purely to close a representation gap if we judge it will put a new reader off reading altogether. Books earn their place as books, and a reader who finishes one good book is more likely to pick up a second.
2. **Public domain, or a free licence compatible with redistribution.** No non-commercial restrictions anywhere in the collection — bundling this collection onto pre-loaded emergency hardware must be unambiguously legal.
3. **Durable literary quality.** Books that have already survived the selection pressure of long reading. The newest title here is *Winnie-the-Pooh* (1926); age is not the point, but survival across generations of readers is.
4. **Varied tone and length.** Novels, short stories, philosophy, poetry, folk tales, and children's books — so that a reader picking a book at random in any mood finds something that fits.
5. **Readable in English today.** Where we've included an old translation, it is because the text repays the effort.

Condition (2) is why the collection stops at around 1926 — the latest US public-domain year when this collection was built (*Winnie-the-Pooh* entered US PD on 1 January 2022). The full account of what we looked for and couldn't bring in is below, under *Modern fiction — the gap, and a challenge*.

## Could we be challenged for *not* including something?

Yes, plausibly, and these gaps matter more than the ones in Scriptures because the constraint is editorial rather than doctrinal:

**"Where are the women?"**
Seven of the twenty-seven works are authored by women — Jane Austen, Mary Shelley, Charlotte and Emily Brontë, Louisa May Alcott, George Eliot, and Kate Chopin. That is still a minority, but a substantial one. Other public-domain women writers we would welcome — Edith Wharton, Willa Cather, Gaskell — are future additions when a contributor brings clean sourcing. Per criterion (1), each addition has to earn its place as a book, not only as a column-filler.

**"Where are the Black and African authors?"**
Three works by Black American authors are now included — Frederick Douglass's *Narrative*, Booker T. Washington's *Up from Slavery*, and W.E.B. Du Bois's *The Souls of Black Folk* — and the *West African Folk Tales* preserve African oral tradition (though through colonial-era editors). Much of the Harlem Renaissance and 20th-century African-American canon (Hurston, Hughes, Ellison, Baldwin) remains under copyright and cannot be added until those works enter the public domain.

**"Where's the modern stuff?"**
Nothing here was published after 1926. This is a consequence of copyright law, not editorial preference — see condition (2) above, and the full account in *Modern fiction — the gap, and a challenge* below. Users with storage are strongly encouraged to add their own modern reads to their Kiwix library.

**"Why so much Western, English-language writing?"**
Also fair. Four non-Western classics are included (*Arabian Nights*, *Shakuntala*, *170 Chinese Poems*, *West African Folk Tales*), but each arrives through a European translator. Translated collections and better in-tradition sourcing are on the roadmap.

## Could what *is* included offend someone?

Honestly, yes — several texts carry the imprint of their era. Transparency seems better than pretending otherwise:

- **Scouting for Boys (Baden-Powell, 1908)** — Baden-Powell has been seriously criticised for pro-Hitler-Youth correspondence in the late 1930s, and for racist and anti-Semitic remarks in his private writing. The 1908 book itself is imperial in tone but focused on outdoor skills, woodcraft, and civic behaviour rather than the more objectionable later material. It is included for its outdoor-competence content, not as an endorsement of its author.
- **The Jungle Book (Kipling)** — Kipling is the canonical case of an imperially-minded writer whose craft is undeniable. The Mowgli stories carry colonial assumptions. Readers should know this going in; Kipling is included for the stories, not for the politics around them.
- **The Arabian Nights (Lang, 1898)** — a heavily sanitised Victorian retelling. Some readers will find its orientalist framing dated. Future releases may add a less mediated version if a usable PD translation can be sourced.
- **West African Folk Tales (Barker & Sinclair, 1917)** — the Anansi stories themselves are authentic, but the editors were British colonial officials in the Gold Coast, and the framing reflects that.
- **Victorian and Edwardian translations** — Giles (Sun Tzu), Long (Marcus Aurelius), FitzGerald (Khayyám), Waley (Chinese poems), Ryder (Shakuntala) are all British or American translators of their era. The translations remain scholarly and readable but reflect the period in which they were made. In every case these were the best PD-licensed renderings we could find; better in-tradition translations are mostly still in copyright.

None of this changes the underlying calculation — a freely-shareable library of humanity's best pre-1926 writing is much better to have than not — but readers deserve to know the provenance of what they are reading.

## A note on size

The whole Literature collection compresses to a few tens of megabytes. Size is not currently a reason for anyone to leave it off their device. If future additions ever push the collection past a few hundred megabytes we'll split it by mood or region so users can choose what to carry.

## Still in sourcing

Four non-Western classics remain on the active sourcing list. Each is genuinely blocked on a technical problem, not on principle:

- **Panchatantra** *(Ryder trans., 1925)* — entered US public domain in 2021 but is not on Project Gutenberg. An archive.org copy needs locating and its copyright page verifying.
- **Journey to the West / Monkey** *(Waley, 1942)* — US public-domain status uncertain and needs a clean legal determination.
- **Condensed Ramayana** *(R.C. Dutt)* — PD certain (d. 1909); needs a clean digital edition located or produced.
- **Gulistan** *(Saadi)* — needs a readable PD English translation. Eastwick (1852) and Ross (1823) are both PD; neither has been digitised to the standard of the rest of this collection. Contributors welcome.

## Modern fiction — the gap, and a challenge

The absence of contemporary fiction in this collection is a legal constraint, not an editorial preference. We searched thoroughly; the gap is real. For the record, here is what we looked for and did not find:

- **Major literary-prize winners, 1975 onwards.** No Booker, Pulitzer, Nobel, National Book Award, or Hugo winner from the last fifty years has been released under a licence that permits free redistribution. All remain under conventional copyright. Authors can dedicate work to the public domain or release it under CC0 voluntarily; a vanishingly small number have.
- **CC-licensed contemporary novels of professional quality.** Every one we located during sourcing — including Cory Doctorow's *Little Brother* and *Walkaway*, Brandon Sanderson's *Warbreaker*, and Peter Watts's *Blindsight* — uses a **non-commercial** variant of Creative Commons (CC BY-NC-SA or CC BY-NC-ND). Including any of them would make this collection non-commercially-licensed as a whole, which blocks legal bundling onto pre-loaded emergency hardware. That bundling friction is the exact thing MComzLibrary exists to eliminate.
- **Unglue.it and similar "unglued books" catalogues.** Some fine CC-licensed works, but at the time of this release nothing under CC0 or CC BY that reaches the readability and quality bar we set for the rest of the collection.

### A challenge

**If you are a working novelist, or you know one:** a serious contemporary novel released under CC0 or CC BY would be a landmark contribution to offline emergency libraries, and to the public domain generally. There are no such books yet. There is plenty of room for the first one. If you have written or published one we have not found, please open an issue on the project.

**If you are, represent, or can reach a potential sponsor of a literary prize:** The Booker, Pulitzer, Nobel, and National Book Award reward excellent contemporary writing — but none require the winning work to be released under a licence that permits free redistribution. We are looking for a sponsor — an individual, foundation, trust, or publisher — to establish a **CC BY Book Prize** modelled on those, with a purse in the region of $100,000 awarded annually to a new novel or work of literary non-fiction on the condition that the winning text is released under CC BY 4.0 or CC0. Each winner would enter this collection permanently — carried, read, and passed on by communities working without reliable internet. In the unlikely but not-inconceivable scenario of severe and irreparable internet failure, each prize-winner would be among the last books to be immortalised as such. We have no prize to offer yet; the idea is genuine. If you can help, please open an issue on the project.

**If you are a reader who owns modern fiction digitally,** the legal friction above does not apply inside your own library. Anything you already own can be added to your personal Kiwix library alongside this collection for your own use. The exclusion here is a distribution constraint — it is not a statement about whether the book is worth reading.

## Longer-term goals

- **Translated collections** in other languages (delivered as `MComz-Literature-ES.zim`, etc.). We especially welcome contributors who can recommend strong public-domain literature in their own language traditions.
- **Broader cultural representation** beyond the Western and English-language default. Non-Western and non-English PD fiction recommendations are warmly invited — see the project README.
- **In-tradition translators.** Where a non-Western text arrives here through a European translator, we hope to add a translation by a writer from within that tradition once a PD version can be sourced.

## A note on what is *not* here

This is not a comprehensive literary canon. It is a starting point: twenty-seven durable works chosen to keep a reader company through difficult hours. Communities with additional storage are warmly encouraged to add the books they love to their own Kiwix library.
