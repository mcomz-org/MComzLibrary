# MComz Scriptures

*Twelve sacred texts from the world's major religious traditions — open the one that speaks to you.*

## Don't panic.

Emergencies are usually all-go. But there are also the other times — times of reflection, times when you want comfort, times when you want to consider the bigger picture beyond what you can see in this physical life. This collection is for those times.

We couldn't include scriptures for every faith — many traditions have no single written scripture. What we have done is assemble the primary scriptures of the world's largest religions that *do* have a single text broadly agreed as central by the majority of adherents, prioritising those we can distribute freely.

## Coverage — with cited numbers

All world-population shares below are quoted directly from the **Pew Research Center's 2012 report *The Global Religious Landscape*** (baseline year 2010, world population 6.9 billion).

| Religion | Adherents (Pew 2010) | Share of world | Central text in this collection |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Christianity | 2.2 billion | 32% | Berean Standard Bible + KJV + World English Bible (with Apocrypha) + Book of Mormon + 1 Enoch (Ethiopian Orthodox) |
| Islam | 1.6 billion | 23% | Quran (Yusuf Ali / Pickthall / Shakir side-by-side) |
| Hinduism | ~1 billion | 15% | Bhagavad Gita (Arnold) + Upanishads (Müller) |
| Buddhism | ~500 million | 7% | Dhammapada (Müller) |
| Folk religions | >400 million | 6% | *(partially — see Confucian and Taoist texts above)* |
| "Other religions" | 58 million | <1% | *(partial — see below)* |
| Judaism | 14 million | 0.2% | JPS Tanakh 1917 |
| Unaffiliated | 1.1 billion | 16% | *not represented — see below* |

Pew groups Bahá'í, Jainism, Sikhism, Shintoism, Taoism, Tenrikyo, Wicca and Zoroastrianism into a single 58 million "other religions" category. Of these, Sikhs account for approximately 25 million (Pew 2015 projection), or about 0.4% of world population.

### What this collection covers

The religions above that have **a single broadly-agreed primary scripture** and which we have **sourced under a free licence** are:

Christianity (across Protestant, Catholic/Orthodox, and LDS canons) + Islam + Hinduism + Buddhism + Judaism + Taoism + Confucian/Chinese ethical tradition

Summing Pew's figures: **32 + 23 + 15 + 7 + 0.2 = 77.2% of world population**, plus Taoism and Confucianism (part of Pew's "folk religions" 6% and "other religions" <1% buckets — exact individual shares are not published by Pew because censuses in much of East Asia do not separate these categories).

**Depending on how East Asian syncretic traditions are attributed, this collection's scriptures are read and revered by somewhere between 77% and 83% of the world's population.**

### The remaining ~1%

Sikhism (~0.4%), Bahá'í (~0.1%), Jainism (~0.06%), and Zoroastrianism (<0.01%) are not yet represented, each for a different reason documented in the "What we hope to add" section below.

### The 16% religiously unaffiliated

By design, this collection does not try to represent atheism, agnosticism, or other forms of non-religion — doing so in a file called *Scriptures* would be a category mistake. The Literature collection carries reflective, philosophical, and consolatory texts written from outside the religious frame:

- **Meditations** *(Marcus Aurelius)* — Stoic practice for bearing difficulty well.
- **Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám** *(FitzGerald trans.)* — Persian verse on impermanence, scepticism, and what to do with a finite life.
- **The Art of War** *(Sun Tzu, Giles trans.)* — Aphoristic strategic philosophy; a natural companion to Meditations.
- **Aesop's Fables** — Ethical instruction without appeal to the divine.

The Tao Te Ching and the Analects in this collection are also widely read by non-religious audiences; both traditions have long atheistic and agnostic reading lineages.

## Where we stopped, and why

We stopped where three conditions converge:

1. **The religion has a single primary scripture** that the majority of adherents would recognise as central.
2. **A freely redistributable English translation exists.**
3. **The tradition represents a meaningful share of the world population** (we drew an informal line near 0.1%, acknowledging it as a working threshold, not a principle).

Condition (1) excludes Shinto, most African traditional religions, most indigenous traditions, and Chinese folk religion in its syncretic form (though its Confucian and Taoist strands are represented here). It also excludes modern neo-pagan movements including Wicca, which explicitly reject the idea of a single revealed scripture.

Condition (2) gates Bahá'í (no suitable free-licence English translation of the *Kitáb-i-Aqdas* exists) and Sikhism (no usable PD English translation of the Guru Granth Sahib exists — the Trumpp 1877 translation is partial and widely considered inaccurate and offensive to Sikhs; the Khalsa translation used by SikhNet is the realistic path, under permissive-grant rather than a standard open licence).

Condition (3) is why Zoroastrianism (<0.01% of world) and Jainism (~0.06%) are not yet included despite both having public-domain English translations readily available — they are on the roadmap rather than omitted in principle.

## Could we be challenged for *not* including something?

Possibly — the honest answer matters more than a defensive one. Three kinds of challenge are foreseeable:

**"Why not Satanism, Wicca, Scientology, or another new religious movement?"**
Principled reasons, not editorial judgment: (a) their primary texts are under active copyright (the *Satanic Bible* 1969, Gardnerian Wiccan texts, Hubbard's works), so we can't legally redistribute them; (b) several lack a single agreed scripture; (c) adherent numbers in each case are well below our 0.1% threshold. Anyone who wants to add these can do so to their own Kiwix library.

**"Why only the Protestant 66-book canon of the Bible?"**
This is a fair challenge and the answer has changed with this release. The Berean Standard Bible and King James Version are both 66-book Protestant canons. Catholic (73 books) and Orthodox (up to 81 books) Christians together make up roughly half of world Christianity. The **World English Bible, Ecumenical Edition** — a modern public-domain translation that includes the full Deuterocanon — is now included to address this gap.

**"Why only the Bible for LDS readers?"**
Likewise addressed. The **Book of Mormon** (1830 edition, public domain) is now included. Pew classifies Latter-day Saints under "Christians" but the Book of Mormon is a distinct scripture and LDS adherents — approximately 17 million worldwide, or 0.25% of world population — meet all three of our inclusion criteria.

## Could what *is* included offend someone?

Honestly, yes — nearly every included text comes with caveats. Transparency seems better than pretending otherwise:

- **Yusuf Ali Quran (1934)** — some mainstream Sunni scholars have historically criticised subtle translation choices; the Saudi-revised 1977 edition removed material the revisers considered problematic. We deliberately use the 1934 original, not the revised edition. Including Pickthall and Shakir alongside mitigates reliance on any one translator. The long-term plan is Arabic alongside.
- **Bhagavad Gita (Arnold, 1885)** — a free-verse Victorian English adaptation by a non-Hindu. Some Hindu readers strongly prefer translations by Hindu scholars (Radhakrishnan, Prabhupada). Arnold is PD; Hindu-authored 20th-century translations are mostly not.
- **Tao Te Ching and Analects (Legge)** — James Legge was a 19th-century Christian missionary to China. Scholars note that his renderings occasionally import Christian theological vocabulary. His translations remain the standard PD scholarly versions.
- **Dhammapada (Müller, 1881)** — F. Max Müller was a German Orientalist working within the "comparative religion" framing of his era. His translation is archaic but academically respected. It will be replaced by the Buddharakkhita translation if BPS grants permission.
- **All included English translations predate 1925** — a consequence of copyright law, not editorial preference. Several reflect the colonial and missionary contexts of their era. More modern and culturally-insider translations exist for most of these texts but are under active copyright. The Berean Standard Bible (2023) and World English Bible (modern) are the only exceptions.

None of these concerns changes the fundamental calculation — a freely-shareable library of humanity's major scriptures is better to have than not — but users should know the provenance of what they are reading. We welcome contributions of better translations wherever licensing permits.

## A note on size

The whole Scriptures collection compresses to a few tens of megabytes — small compared to the ~4 GB core MComzOS image and very small compared to WikiMed Mini (~155 MB) or the full Wikipedia download (~110 GB). Size is not currently a reason for anyone to leave this collection off their device. We'll keep a careful eye on this as we add future texts: a scripture library that grows past roughly 200 MB might start affecting which users choose to include it, so future additions will be weighed against that.

## Longer-term goals

- **Side-by-side original-language editions.** Arabic alongside the Yusuf Ali Quran, Hebrew alongside the JPS Tanakh, Sanskrit alongside the Arnold Gita, Pali alongside the Dhammapada, and Classical Chinese alongside the Tao Te Ching and Analects, as original-language digital sources become available. Stretch goal for v1.0.
- **Translated collections.** These texts exist in dozens of languages; the Quran, Bible and Dhammapada are among the most-translated books on earth. We welcome contributors — see the project README.

## A note on what is *not* here

This is not a comprehensive religious library. It is a starting point: one widely-read primary text (or side-by-side pair) per tradition, chosen so that someone who reaches for comfort, reflection, or a bigger-picture view finds something that speaks to them. Communities with additional storage are encouraged to add commentaries, additional translations, and texts from traditions we had to set aside, to their Kiwix library.
