# MComzOS offline library: content audit and expansion report

The most important finding across all five research tasks is that **virtually every major WASH and humanitarian publication uses NC-restricted licences**, making the water/sanitation gap the hardest to fill. However, two outstanding discoveries change the picture: the US Public Health Service's *Ship's Medicine Chest and Medical Aid at Sea* (public domain, designed for laypeople providing prolonged care without a doctor) and the **WEDC Guides** (claimed CC BY 4.0, though verification is needed). For literature, Arthur Ryder's *Panchatantra* and FitzGerald's *Rubáiyát* are immediate, high-quality additions. For agriculture, the **USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning** is the single most practical find. Modern CC-licensed fiction under non-NC terms is effectively nonexistent — every quality CC novel uses NC variants.

## 3. Ship Captain's Medical Guide findings

### Status of the 22nd edition

The **22nd edition was published in 1998/1999** by The Stationery Office (TSO) for the Maritime and Coastguard Agency (MCA), under **Crown Copyright 1999** (ISBN 0-11-551658-1). It contains 232 pages across 13 chapters plus annexes.

Two copies exist on Internet Archive: a freely downloadable scan tagged "Public Domain Mark 1.0" and a controlled-lending library scan. The guide was **hosted on GOV.UK from May 2014 until October 2019** as individually downloadable chapter PDFs, under the standard GOV.UK footer stating "All content is available under the Open Government Licence v3.0, except where otherwise stated." No exceptions were noted for the 22nd edition content. The page was withdrawn when the 23rd edition launched, but **OGL is non-exclusive, perpetual, and irrevocable** — withdrawal does not revoke the licence.

### OGL assessment

The 22nd edition **appears redistributable under OGL v3.0** based on three factors: it is Crown Copyright with no identified third-party IP; it was published on GOV.UK under the default OGL footer without stated exceptions; and the government explicitly noted that only the **23rd and 24th editions** (which were "complete rewrites" with ~20 external contributors) contain third-party IP preventing redistribution. Marine Information Notice MIN 718 for the 24th edition specifically states: "Due to intellectual property included in the latest edition, the SCMG is not freely available from www.gov.uk." This language implicitly distinguishes the newer editions from the 22nd, which *was* freely available.

**Caveat**: The physical book's copyright page should be inspected for any third-party acknowledgements. The Internet Archive "Public Domain Mark 1.0" designation is technically incorrect — it is OGL-licensed Crown Copyright, not public domain.

### How the SCMG compares to the SOF Medical Handbook

These two guides serve fundamentally different audiences and are **complementary, not competing**.

The SCMG is written for **ship's officers with no medical training** — "intelligent laymen" who must manage illness and injury when days or weeks from port. It uses plain language, symptom-based diagnosis, and explicitly limits itself to procedures laypeople can safely perform. It includes **18 pages on general nursing** (ongoing patient monitoring, bed care, vitals), which is exactly the prolonged-care gap the library needs to fill. It covers childbirth, mental health, communicable diseases, survivor care, and even end-of-life management ("The Dying and the Dead"). It does **not** cover surgery.

The SOF Medical Handbook targets **highly trained Special Operations medics** (18D MOS). It is far more comprehensive medically — **600+ pages** covering organ systems, parasitology, pharmacology, field surgical procedures, veterinary medicine, and CBRN injuries. But it explicitly states it has "limited application outside of the SOF context." A layperson opening the SOF handbook during an emergency would find it overwhelming; a layperson opening the SCMG would find actionable guidance.

**Recommendation**: Include both. The SCMG fills the critical gap between basic first aid (FM 4-25.11) and advanced medical reference (SOF handbook). For the same reason, the US *Ship's Medicine Chest and Medical Aid at Sea* (2003, DHHS, public domain) should also be included — it serves the same layperson-in-prolonged-isolation scenario from the American side, with no copyright restrictions whatsoever.

## 4. Water bibliography assessment

Martin's instinct that most of his bibliography is too technical is **partially wrong**. Several of his sources are specifically designed for non-specialist humanitarian field workers, not hydraulic engineers. The humanitarian WASH sector explicitly produces resources at field-worker reading level because end users are generalists, volunteers, and community leaders.

Worth the effort — these are among the most practically useful WASH resources for emergencies |

Wagner & Lanoix is a credible backup if modern alternatives aren't available, but the WEDC Guides supersede it on every dimension.

## Critical next steps

Three actions would significantly strengthen the library:

**First, verify the WEDC Guides licence.** The WEDC collection document explicitly states CC BY 4.0, but individual items in Loughborough's Figshare repository show CC BY-NC-ND 4.0. This contradiction must be resolved by contacting WEDC directly. If confirmed as CC BY 4.0, the WEDC Guides become the **cornerstone of the entire water/sanitation section** — they are the only openly-licensed, layperson-accessible, practically useful WASH resources identified in this entire audit.

**Second, verify the copyright renewal status of Waley's *Monkey*** (1942) using the US Copyright Office renewal records. If the copyright was not renewed (which specialist analysis suggests is likely), this is a PD text of outstanding quality that fills a major gap in non-Western literature.

**Third, contact Oxfam and the Sphere Association** to request redistribution permission for emergency preparedness use. Both organisations' missions align with offline emergency knowledge access. The Oxfam WASH Briefs in particular are the most practically useful water/sanitation resources assessed, and permission for non-commercial educational distribution seems plausible given Oxfam's humanitarian mandate.
